Section 01Mission Statement

"To preserve life, reduce suffering, and provide compassionate emergency medical care to all residents and visitors of San Andreas, without discrimination or prejudice."

The Emergency Medical Services (EMS) division, operating under Pillbox Medical Center, is the primary emergency medical response agency for San Andreas. EMS personnel provide pre-hospital emergency care, interfacility transport, and emergency department services. Unlike law enforcement, EMS operates under a medical model focused on patient care, not criminal enforcement.

EMS responders are expected to remain calm under pressure, provide competent medical care, and treat every patient with dignity and respect. Whether responding to a gunshot wound in the hood, a heart attack in a mansion, or an overdose on the streets, every patient deserves our best care.

1.1 Core Values

  • Compassion — EMS personnel treat patients in their most vulnerable moments. Show empathy, kindness, and respect.
  • Competence — Medical skills must be maintained through ongoing training. Lives depend on your knowledge.
  • Courage — EMS enters dangerous scenes (shootings, fires, hazmat). Assess risks but do not abandon patients.
  • Confidentiality — Patient medical information is protected by HIPAA. Do NOT share patient details outside of medical necessity.
  • Teamwork — Coordinate with LEO, fire, hospital staff. Egos have no place in emergency medicine.

1.2 Oath of Service

All EMS personnel shall, upon certification, take the following oath:

"I solemnly swear to use my medical knowledge and skills for the benefit of the sick and injured, to maintain the highest standards of care, to respect the dignity of every patient, and to uphold the honor of the medical profession. I will not discriminate based on race, religion, gender, criminal history, or ability to pay. I will protect patient confidentiality and act always in the patient's best interest. So help me God."

Section 02Chain of Command

EMS operates under a medical hierarchy where clinical decisions are made based on certification level, not rank. However, administrative and operational authority follows the chain of command.

2.1 Rank Structure & Authority

Rank Callsign Authority Pay Grade
Medical Director Medic-1 Chief of EMS. Must be licensed physician (MD). Medical protocol authority, hiring/termination, budget, inter-agency agreements. Grade 7
Assistant Director Medic-2 Operations manager. Scheduling, vehicle maintenance, supplies, disciplinary authority. Assumes Medical Director authority in absence. Grade 6
Supervisor Supervisor-[Unit#] Shift commander. Scene medical authority on multi-unit calls. Performance reviews. FTO oversight. Must hold ALS or MD certification. Grade 5
Senior Paramedic Medic-[Unit#] FTO certified. Advanced procedures. Mentors junior personnel. May act as scene commander in Supervisor's absence. Must hold ALS cert. Grade 4
Paramedic Medic-[Unit#] Full ALS treatment authority. Advanced airway, IV medications, cardiac monitoring. Minimum 1 year service for promotion eligibility. Grade 3
EMT EMT-[Unit#] BLS treatment authority. Basic airway, CPR, bleeding control, splinting. May drive ambulance. Minimum 6 months for promotion to Paramedic. Grade 2
Trainee Trainee-[ID] In academy or field training. Must be supervised by FTO. No independent patient care authority. Grade 1

2.2 Medical Command Authority

On scene with multiple EMS units, the highest medical certification (MD > ALS > BLS) has medical command authority. This may differ from administrative rank. Example: A Senior Paramedic (ALS) may assume medical command over a Supervisor (BLS) for clinical decisions.

2.3 Reporting Structure

  1. All personnel report to their shift Supervisor.
  2. Supervisors report to Assistant Director.
  3. Assistant Director reports to Medical Director.
  4. Medical Director reports to Pillbox Hospital Administration and State Department of Health.

Section 03Certification Levels & Scope of Practice

All EMS personnel must hold a valid state certification. Exceeding your scope of practice is grounds for termination and loss of certification.

3.1 Basic Life Support (BLS) — EMT Certification

Training: 120-hour EMT course + state practical exam

Authorized Procedures:

  • Airway: Oropharyngeal airway (OPA), nasopharyngeal airway (NPA), bag-valve-mask (BVM) ventilation
  • Breathing: Supplemental oxygen (nasal cannula, non-rebreather mask), pulse oximetry
  • Circulation: CPR, AED, bleeding control (direct pressure, tourniquets)
  • Medications: Aspirin (cardiac), nitroglycerin (patient's own), epinephrine auto-injector (patient's own), naloxone (Narcan) for overdose, glucose (oral)
  • Splinting: Fracture immobilization, spinal immobilization (backboard, C-collar)
  • Vitals: BP, pulse, respirations, SpO2, blood glucose (glucometer)

Prohibited: IV access, advanced airways, cardiac drugs, surgical procedures

3.2 Advanced Life Support (ALS) — Paramedic Certification

Training: 2-year paramedic program + national registry exam

Authorized Procedures (in addition to BLS):

  • Airway: Endotracheal intubation, supraglottic airways (King LT, i-gel), cricothyrotomy (emergency surgical airway)
  • Circulation: IV/IO access, saline/LR fluid resuscitation, blood pressure management
  • Cardiac: 12-lead ECG, cardiac monitoring, defibrillation (manual), cardioversion, external pacing, ACLS drugs (epinephrine, atropine, amiodarone, lidocaine)
  • Medications: Full formulary (cardiac drugs, pain management, sedation, paralytics, vasopressors)
  • Procedures: Needle decompression (tension pneumothorax), chest tube placement (MD supervision), central line placement (MD supervision)

3.3 Medical Doctor (MD) — Physician License

Training: Medical school + residency + state medical board license

Authorized Procedures: All BLS/ALS procedures + surgical procedures, pronouncement of death, advanced trauma life support, code blue leadership

Role: Medical Director oversees all EMS operations. MDs on shift provide direct patient care and supervise complex cases.

Scope Violation: Performing procedures beyond your certification level is a criminal act (practicing medicine without a license) and will result in immediate termination, loss of certification, and possible criminal prosecution.

Section 04Emergency Response Procedures

4.1 Dispatch Priority Levels

Priority Response Code Description Examples
Priority 1 Code 3 Life-threatening emergency. Lights & siren. Immediate response. Expect <5 min ETA urban, <10 min rural. Cardiac arrest, active shooting victim, unresponsive, severe trauma, drowning, anaphylaxis
Priority 2 Code 2 Urgent but not immediately life-threatening. Expedited response, lights only (no siren). <10 min ETA urban, <15 min rural. Fractures, lacerations, vehicle accident with injuries, chest pain (stable), difficulty breathing (stable)
Priority 3 Code 1 Non-emergency. Routine response, no lights/siren. <20 min ETA. Minor injuries, welfare check, non-emergency transport, lift assist, patient refusal follow-up

4.2 Response Procedure

  1. Dispatch: Acknowledge call immediately: "Medic-[unit] responding Code [1/2/3] to [location]"
  2. En Route: Don BSI (gloves minimum). Review dispatch notes. Request LEO if scene potentially unsafe.
  3. Arrival: Notify dispatch: "Medic-[unit] on scene". Perform scene size-up (safety, mechanism, number of patients).
  4. Scene Safety: If scene unsafe (active shooter, fire, hazmat, unstable vehicle), stage 1 block away and wait for scene to be secured.
  5. Patient Contact: Introduce yourself: "Hi, I'm [Name] with EMS. What's going on today?" Begin assessment.

4.3 Emergency Driving

Code 3 driving does NOT exempt you from traffic laws. You are responsible for every collision. Proceed with caution, especially through intersections.

  • Activate emergency lights and siren for Code 3 responses.
  • Slow down at all red lights and stop signs. Proceed only when safe.
  • Do NOT exceed 15 mph over posted speed limit (urban) or 20 mph (highway).
  • If traffic does not yield, STOP. Do not force your way through.
  • All collisions must be reported immediately to Supervisor. Automatic IA investigation.

Section 05Patient Assessment & Triage

5.1 ABCDE Primary Assessment

Perform on EVERY patient, in order. Life threats are addressed immediately before moving to next step.

  1. A — Airway: Is the airway open and clear? If obstructed (tongue, blood, vomit), open airway (head-tilt-chin-lift or jaw thrust if spinal injury suspected).
  2. B — Breathing: Is the patient breathing? Rate, depth, effort? Auscultate lung sounds. Apply oxygen if SpO2 <94%. Assist ventilations if inadequate.
  3. C — Circulation: Pulse present? Rate, strength, regularity? Check for external bleeding (apply direct pressure/tourniquet). Skin color/temp (pale/cool = shock).
  4. D — Disability: Neurological status. AVPU scale (Alert, Verbal response, Pain response, Unresponsive). Pupils equal/reactive? GCS score if head trauma.
  5. E — Exposure: Remove clothing to examine entire body (maintain patient dignity with blankets). Look for hidden injuries (stab wounds, burns).

5.2 SAMPLE History

Obtain from patient (or bystanders if patient unable):

  • S — Signs & Symptoms: What is bothering you? Pain (OPQRST: Onset, Provokes, Quality, Radiates, Severity 1-10, Time)?
  • A — Allergies: Any medication or environmental allergies?
  • M — Medications: What medications do you take? Include recreational drugs.
  • P — Past Medical History: Any chronic conditions? Prior surgeries?
  • L — Last Oral Intake: When did you last eat/drink? (Important for surgery/anesthesia)
  • E — Events Leading Up: What were you doing when this started?

5.3 Vital Signs

Obtain and document vitals on every patient:

Vital Normal Range (Adult) Critical Values
Heart Rate 60-100 bpm <40 (bradycardia) or >120 (tachycardia)
Blood Pressure 90/60 to 120/80 mmHg <90 systolic (shock) or >180 systolic (hypertensive crisis)
Respirations 12-20 breaths/min <10 (respiratory failure) or >30 (respiratory distress)
SpO2 95-100% <90% (hypoxia — apply oxygen)
Blood Glucose 70-110 mg/dL <60 (hypoglycemia) or >300 (DKA)

5.4 Triage (Multiple Patients)

When patients exceed available resources, use START Triage to prioritize:

Category Color Criteria Treatment Priority
Immediate RED Life-threatening but survivable with immediate intervention (e.g., severe bleeding, airway obstruction, tension pneumothorax) 1st
Delayed YELLOW Serious injuries but can tolerate 30-60 min delay (e.g., fractures, moderate burns, stable chest pain) 2nd
Minor GREEN Walking wounded. Minor injuries (e.g., lacerations, sprains). Can self-evacuate. 3rd
Deceased BLACK Dead or injuries incompatible with life (e.g., decapitation, massive head trauma, asystole for >20 min) None (tag and move to morgue area)

Section 06Treatment Protocols

6.1 Cardiac Arrest Protocol

Time-sensitive emergency. Begin CPR immediately. Every second counts.

  1. Confirm Arrest: Unresponsive + no pulse. Call for backup unit and supervisor.
  2. CPR: 30 chest compressions (2 inches deep, 100-120/min) : 2 ventilations. Continuous until ALS arrival or ROSC.
  3. AED: Apply as soon as available. Analyze rhythm. Shock if advised. Resume CPR immediately after shock.
  4. ALS Interventions: Advanced airway (ET tube), IV/IO access, epinephrine 1mg IV q3-5min, amiodarone 300mg IV if VF/pulseless VT.
  5. ROSC (Return of Spontaneous Circulation): Check pulse after 2 min CPR. If pulse present, transport immediately to Pillbox.
  6. Termination: If no ROSC after 20 min and no shockable rhythm, consider termination (Supervisor or MD approval required).

6.2 Gunshot Wound (GSW) / Penetrating Trauma

Scene safety first. Ensure LEO has secured scene before approaching. Gunshot wounds are crime scenes — preserve evidence when possible.

  1. Hemorrhage Control: Direct pressure to wound. If extremity bleed and pressure fails, apply tourniquet 2-3 inches above wound. Note time applied.
  2. Chest Wounds: Occlusive dressing (3-sided tape or chest seal). Monitor for tension pneumothorax (decreased breath sounds, JVD, hypotension). If present, needle decompression (ALS only).
  3. Abdominal Wounds: Cover evisceration with moist sterile dressing. Do NOT push organs back in.
  4. Spinal Precautions: If mechanism suggests spinal injury, full immobilization (C-collar, backboard).
  5. IV Access (ALS): 2 large-bore IVs. Fluid resuscitation (maintain systolic BP >90 mmHg). Do NOT delay transport for IVs — start en route.
  6. Transport: Rapid transport to Pillbox trauma bay. Notify hospital of GSW patient with ETA. Minimize on-scene time (<10 min).

6.3 Motor Vehicle Accident (MVA)

  1. Scene Safety: Park upwind/uphill if hazmat suspected. Activate emergency lights. Wear reflective vest. Request fire department for extrication if patient trapped.
  2. Spinal Immobilization: All high-speed MVA patients get full spinal precautions unless all of the following are true: ambulatory at scene, no LOC, no spinal pain, no distracting injuries, no intoxication.
  3. Assessment: Check all occupants (even if they claim they're fine). Airbag deployment = significant force. Look for seatbelt sign (bruising across chest).
  4. Extrication: If patient trapped, fire department performs extrication. EMS provides in-vehicle medical care (C-collar, oxygen) while extrication ongoing.
  5. Transport Decision: Any of the following = immediate transport: chest pain, difficulty breathing, altered mental status, significant bleeding, ejection from vehicle.

6.4 Overdose / Poisoning

  • Opioid Overdose (Heroin, Fentanyl): Pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression (<10/min), unresponsive. Administer naloxone (Narcan) 2mg intranasal or 0.4mg IV. May repeat q2-3min if no response. Support airway/ventilations. Transport.
  • Stimulant Overdose (Cocaine, Meth): Agitation, hyperthermia, tachycardia, seizures. Cool patient if hyperthermic. Benzodiazepines (midazolam 5mg IM/IV) for seizures or severe agitation (ALS only). Transport.
  • Alcohol Intoxication: Check blood glucose (hypoglycemia common). If unresponsive, assume other drugs involved. Airway management. Transport if unable to protect airway or signs of alcohol poisoning.

6.5 Diabetic Emergencies

Condition Signs/Symptoms Treatment
Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar) Blood glucose <60 mg/dL. Altered mental status, diaphoresis, shaking, seizures. If conscious and can swallow: oral glucose (tube of gel). If unconscious or unable to swallow: dextrose 25g IV (ALS) or glucagon 1mg IM. Recheck glucose after 10 min.
Hyperglycemia / DKA Blood glucose >300 mg/dL. Fruity breath odor, Kussmaul respirations, dehydration, altered mental status. IV fluid resuscitation (saline 500mL bolus). Continuous IV fluids en route. Do NOT give insulin in field. Transport to Pillbox.

Section 07Transport Procedures

7.1 Transport Decision

All patients assessed by EMS should be transported to Pillbox Medical Center unless:

  • Patient refuses transport (must be alert, oriented, and sign refusal form)
  • Patient pronounced dead on scene (Paramedic+ or MD after 20 min resuscitation or obvious death)
  • Patient treated and released by MD on scene (special events with medical tent)

7.2 Transport Notification

Notify dispatch when transporting:

  • "Medic-[unit] transporting Priority [1/2/3] to Pillbox Medical Center, ETA [X] minutes"
  • For Priority 1 (critical) patients, also notify Pillbox directly via radio or phone with brief report (SBAR format).

7.3 Patient Packaging

  • All Patients: Loaded on ambulance stretcher. Secured with 3 straps minimum. Seatbelt over patient. Side rails up.
  • Spinal Immobilization: C-collar + backboard + straps. Head blocks or towel rolls to prevent lateral movement. Secure to stretcher.
  • Cardiac Monitor: All Priority 1 patients on cardiac monitor (ALS unit). Continuous SpO2 monitoring.
  • IV Access: Secured with tape or IV shield. Run fluids through ambulance IV pole (not free-hanging bag).

7.4 En Route Care

  • Driver: Focus on safe driving. Code 3 transport only if Priority 1 AND time-sensitive (e.g., STEMI, stroke, trauma).
  • Attendant: Remain in patient compartment. Continuous monitoring. Reassess vitals q5min (critical) or q15min (stable). Document all interventions.
  • Updates: If patient condition changes (improves or deteriorates), notify driver and hospital immediately.

7.5 Hospital Handoff (SBAR Report)

Upon arrival at Pillbox, provide SBAR report to receiving nurse/physician:

  • S — Situation: "This is [patient name/age], chief complaint is [e.g., chest pain]."
  • B — Background: "Patient has history of [medical conditions], takes [medications], allergies to [drugs]."
  • A — Assessment: "Vitals are [BP/HR/RR/SpO2/BG]. Physical exam shows [findings]. We administered [treatments]."
  • R — Recommendation: "Patient needs [e.g., trauma bay, cardiac cath lab, etc.]."

Transfer patient to hospital bed. Provide patient care report (PCR) to hospital staff. Obtain receiving nurse/physician signature.

Section 08Mass Casualty Incident Protocol

MCI Declared When: Patients exceed available EMS resources (typically 5+ patients, or 3+ Priority 1 patients).

8.1 MCI Activation

  1. First Arriving Unit: Performs scene size-up. Estimates number of patients. Declares MCI over radio: "Dispatch, Medic-[unit] declaring MCI at [location]. Approximately [X] patients. Requesting all available EMS units and supervisor."
  2. Dispatch: Activates MCI protocol. Dispatches all available units. Notifies Pillbox to prepare for mass casualty.
  3. Establish Command: First arriving Supervisor assumes Incident Commander role. Sets up command post. Dons high-visibility vest.

8.2 ICS Structure for MCI

  • Incident Commander (IC): Supervisor or highest ranking EMS on scene. Overall authority. Coordinates with LEO/Fire.
  • Triage Officer: Performs START triage on all patients. Tags patients with color-coded ribbons (red/yellow/green/black).
  • Treatment Officer: Manages treatment area. Assigns patients to available EMS personnel.
  • Transport Officer: Coordinates ambulance staging. Assigns patients to ambulances. Notifies hospitals of incoming patients.
  • Staging Officer: Manages ambulance staging area (upwind/uphill from scene). Directs arriving units to triage, treatment, or transport.

8.3 START Triage (30-Second Assessment)

  1. Walking Wounded: Call out: "Anyone who can walk, move to [safe area]." Tag all as GREEN.
  2. For Non-Ambulatory:
    • Respirations: Breathing? If NO, open airway. Still not breathing? Tag BLACK. If YES, count respirations. >30/min or <10/min? Tag RED. Otherwise continue.
    • Perfusion: Check radial pulse or capillary refill. Absent radial pulse or cap refill >2 sec? Tag RED. Otherwise continue.
    • Mental Status: Can patient follow simple commands ("Squeeze my hand")? If NO, tag RED. If YES, tag YELLOW.
  3. Move to Next Patient. Do NOT begin treatment during triage phase.

8.4 Treatment Area

Once triage complete, patients moved to treatment area by color:

  • RED Area: Immediate treatment (airway, hemorrhage control, IV access). Transport priority #1.
  • YELLOW Area: Delayed treatment. Monitor for deterioration. Transport after all RED patients.
  • GREEN Area: Minimal treatment. Self-care if possible. Transport last (may use buses for mass transport).
  • BLACK Area: Covered and moved to separate area. LEO handles body recovery and identification.

Section 09Medication Administration

Medication Errors Can Be Fatal. Verify drug name, dose, route, and patient allergies before administering ANY medication. When in doubt, contact Medical Director.

9.1 Six Rights of Medication Administration

  1. Right Patient: Confirm patient identity (name, DOB if known).
  2. Right Drug: Read label 3 times (when selecting, when preparing, when administering).
  3. Right Dose: Calculate based on patient weight if required. Double-check math.
  4. Right Route: IV, IM, sublingual, inhalation, etc. Ensure correct route for drug.
  5. Right Time: Is this the appropriate time to give this drug?
  6. Right Documentation: Document drug name, dose, route, time, and patient response immediately.

9.2 BLS Medication Formulary

Drug Indication Dose Route
Aspirin Chest pain (suspected MI) 324 mg (4 baby aspirin, chewed) PO
Nitroglycerin Chest pain (if patient has own prescription) 0.4 mg (1 tablet sublingual or spray) SL
Naloxone (Narcan) Opioid overdose 2 mg (may repeat q3min) Intranasal or IM
Epinephrine Auto-Injector Anaphylaxis (if patient has own EpiPen) 0.3 mg (adult) or 0.15 mg (pediatric) IM (lateral thigh)
Oral Glucose Hypoglycemia (conscious patient) 15g (1 tube glucose gel) PO (between cheek and gum)
Oxygen Hypoxia (SpO2 <94%) 2-15 L/min (titrate to SpO2 94-99%) Inhalation (nasal cannula or NRB mask)

9.3 ALS Medication Formulary (Paramedic Only)

Drug Indication Dose Route
Epinephrine Cardiac arrest, anaphylaxis 1 mg q3-5min (arrest), 0.3mg IM (anaphylaxis) IV/IO or IM
Atropine Bradycardia (HR <50 with symptoms) 0.5 mg IV, may repeat to 3 mg total IV/IO
Amiodarone VF/pulseless VT (cardiac arrest) 300 mg IV, may repeat 150 mg once IV/IO
Dextrose 25% Hypoglycemia (unconscious) 25 g (100mL) IV over 2 min IV
Midazolam (Versed) Seizures, severe agitation 5 mg IM/IV, may repeat once IM or IV
Morphine Severe pain (trauma, MI) 2-4 mg IV q5min (titrate to pain <5/10) IV
Fentanyl Severe pain (faster onset than morphine) 1 mcg/kg IV (max 100 mcg), may repeat IV
Dopamine Hypotension (shock) 5-20 mcg/kg/min IV drip (titrate to BP) IV infusion

9.4 Medication Error Reporting

If a medication error occurs (wrong drug, wrong dose, wrong patient), immediately:

  1. Notify partner and Supervisor.
  2. Monitor patient closely for adverse reaction.
  3. Notify receiving hospital of medication error.
  4. File medication error report within 24 hours.
  5. Automatic review by Medical Director. May result in remedial training or suspension depending on severity.

Section 11Scene Safety & LEO Coordination

YOU CANNOT HELP ANYONE IF YOU ARE DEAD. Scene safety is always the first priority. Never enter an unsafe scene. Wait for LEO to secure.

11.1 Hot/Warm/Cold Zones

Zone Description EMS Action
HOT Zone Active threat present (active shooter, fire, hazmat, unstable building). Immediate danger to life. DO NOT ENTER. Stage at safe distance (1+ blocks). Wait for LEO/Fire to declare scene safe.
WARM Zone Threat neutralized but not fully secured. LEO still clearing scene. Potential for secondary threats. Enter ONLY with LEO escort. Wear ballistic vest if available. Be prepared to evacuate if scene becomes unsafe.
COLD Zone Scene fully secured by LEO. No active threats. Safe for EMS operations. Proceed with normal patient care. Maintain situational awareness — scenes can escalate.

11.2 Tactical EMS (Active Shooter / Barricaded Suspect)

  • EMS stages in COLD zone (typically 1-2 blocks from scene).
  • LEO SWAT/patrol forms rescue task force (RTF): 2-4 LEO + 2 EMS (ballistic vests, minimal equipment).
  • RTF enters WARM zone to extract wounded victims to COLD zone where full treatment provided.
  • EMS does NOT enter HOT zone. LEO drags victims out of hot zone, then EMS takes over.

11.3 Crime Scene Preservation

EMS has priority to save lives, but must preserve evidence when possible:

  • Avoid disturbing weapons, shell casings, blood spatter.
  • If must move patient, note original position.
  • Do NOT cut through bullet holes or stab wounds when removing clothing (cut around).
  • Preserve all clothing (bag separately, do not shake out). Give to LEO.
  • If patient dies in ambulance, consider ambulance a crime scene. Notify LEO. Do not clean until LEO photographs interior.

11.4 Violent/Combative Patients

Request LEO before approaching violent or potentially violent patients. LEO will restrain patient, then EMS provides medical care.

  • Signs of violence: Clenched fists, pacing, yelling, throwing objects, paranoid statements.
  • De-escalation: Calm voice, non-threatening posture, offer choices, give patient space (6+ feet).
  • If patient attacks, RETREAT. Do not fight. Call for LEO immediately.
  • Once LEO restrains patient, assess for injuries from restraint (positional asphyxia if prone, taser barbs, etc.).
  • Chemical sedation (midazolam 5mg IM) authorized for violent psych patients AFTER LEO restraint and Supervisor approval.

Section 12Mental Health & Psychiatric Emergencies

12.1 Psych Patient Assessment

Approach: "I'm [Name] with EMS. I'm here to help. What's going on today?"

  • Risk Assessment: Is patient a danger to self or others? Suicidal ideation (plan/means)? Homicidal ideation?
  • Medical Clearance: Many "psych" calls have medical causes (hypoglycemia, hypoxia, head injury, drugs). Check vitals, blood glucose, pupils.
  • Organic vs Functional: Sudden onset behavioral change in elderly = stroke/infection/delirium (MEDICAL). Long psych history = functional psych (PSYCH).

12.2 Suicidal Patient

Take ALL suicide threats seriously. "I'm going to kill myself" = immediate psych hold. Do NOT leave patient alone.

  1. Remove Means: Ask patient to hand over pills, weapons, belts, shoelaces. Have LEO secure if patient refuses.
  2. Constant Supervision: EMS or LEO with patient at all times. Suicidal patients have been known to jump from moving ambulances.
  3. Calm Environment: Low stimulation. Soft voice. No sudden movements. Explain everything you're doing.
  4. 5150 Hold: Paramedic+ or LEO can place patient on 72-hour involuntary psych hold if danger to self/others or gravely disabled. Transport to Pillbox psych ward.
  5. Medical Director Approval: All 5150 holds require Medical Director notification within 1 hour.

12.3 Agitated Psych Patient

  • De-escalation: First line. Calm voice, validate feelings, offer choices. "I can see you're upset. How can I help?"
  • Positioning: Maintain exit route for yourself. Do NOT corner patient. Keep 6+ feet distance.
  • LEO Assistance: If de-escalation fails or patient becomes threatening, request LEO.
  • Physical Restraint: ONLY if patient is immediate danger to self/others AND de-escalation failed. LEO performs restraint. EMS does NOT physically restrain (risk of injury).
  • Chemical Sedation: Midazolam (Versed) 5mg IM for severely agitated patient who is restrained and poses ongoing danger. Requires Supervisor or MD approval. Monitor airway closely (respiratory depression).

12.4 Overdose with Psych Component

Intentional overdose (suicide attempt) requires both medical and psych treatment:

  1. Treat medical emergency first (airway, naloxone, IV fluids, etc.).
  2. Once medically stable, patient on 5150 psych hold (cannot refuse transport).
  3. Transport to Pillbox. Medical treatment first (detox, stabilization), then psych evaluation.
  4. Notify hospital that patient is on 5150 hold (security may be needed if patient tries to leave).

Section 13Uniform & Vehicle Standards

13.1 Uniform Requirements

Item Specification
Shirt EMS polo or button-up shirt (navy blue or white). EMS patch on both shoulders. Name tag above right pocket. Rank insignia on collar.
Pants Navy blue cargo pants or EMS uniform pants. No jeans, shorts, or sweatpants.
Footwear Black boots or shoes. Closed-toe, slip-resistant, leather or synthetic. No sneakers, sandals, or open-toe shoes.
Outerwear EMS-issued jacket or fleece (navy blue with EMS logo). High-visibility vest for highway calls.

Grooming Clean, professional appearance. Hair neat (long hair tied back for infection control). Nails short (no acrylics — interfere with glove fit). Minimal jewelry (rings/watches removed during patient care). No visible tattoos depicting violence, profanity, or hate symbols.

13.2 Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Mandatory PPE based on call type:

  • All Calls: Nitrile gloves (size appropriate, two pairs if dealing with blood/fluids).
  • Respiratory Calls: N95 mask (TB, COVID, influenza suspected).
  • Blood/Fluid Exposure: Gloves + eye protection (safety glasses or face shield) + gown if splatter likely.
  • Hazmat: Do NOT enter hazmat scene. Stage upwind. Decon performed by fire department.
  • Highway Calls: High-visibility reflective vest (ANSI Class 2 or 3). Mandatory on all freeway/highway responses.

13.3 Vehicle Standards

  • Ambulance Types:
    • BLS Ambulance: Staffed with 2 EMTs. Basic equipment (AED, oxygen, splints, backboard).
    • ALS Ambulance: Staffed with at least 1 Paramedic + 1 EMT. Advanced equipment (cardiac monitor, IV supplies, full drug box).
  • Daily Vehicle Check: Before shift, inspect ambulance: fuel, oil, lights/siren, oxygen tanks (full), suction (working), stretcher (functional), all equipment present and stocked.
  • Cleanliness: Ambulance cleaned after every patient transport. Blood/fluid spills cleaned immediately with hospital-grade disinfectant. Full deep-clean weekly.
  • Restocking: Restock all used supplies immediately after hospital drop-off (gauze, IV supplies, drugs). Never leave station with incomplete stock.

Section 14Documentation & Patient Care Reports

"If it's not documented, it didn't happen." PCRs are legal documents. They can be subpoenaed in court. Write clearly, accurately, and objectively.

14.1 PCR Requirements

Patient Care Report (PCR) must be completed for EVERY patient contact, even if no transport:

  • Patient Demographics: Name, age/DOB, gender, address, phone number (if obtainable).
  • Chief Complaint: Why did patient call 911? Use patient's own words in quotes ("My chest hurts").
  • History: SAMPLE history (see Section 5.2). Past medical history, medications, allergies.
  • Vital Signs: At least 2 sets of vitals (initial and after treatment). Include BP, HR, RR, SpO2, BG if checked.
  • Assessment Findings: Physical exam findings. AVPU or GCS. Lung sounds, heart sounds, abdominal exam, skin findings.
  • Treatment: EVERY intervention documented with time. Oxygen applied, IVs started, medications given (drug/dose/route/time), CPR (start/stop times), splinting, etc.
  • Transport: Destination hospital, mode (Code 1/2/3), patient condition on arrival.
  • Handoff: Name of receiving nurse/physician. Signature obtained.

14.2 Refusal of Care

If patient refuses treatment or transport, documentation is CRITICAL (liability protection):

  1. Medical Screening Exam (MSE): Even if patient refuses, obtain vitals and brief assessment. Inform patient of risks of not going to hospital.
  2. Decision-Making Capacity: Patient must be alert, oriented (person/place/time/event), and understand risks. If intoxicated, altered mental status, or under 18 years old, patient CANNOT refuse.
  3. Against Medical Advice (AMA) Form: Patient signs AMA form acknowledging risks. If patient refuses to sign, document "patient verbally refused transport and refused to sign AMA form" and have witness (LEO, family member) sign.
  4. PCR Documentation: Document MSE findings, explanation of risks given, patient's stated reason for refusal, patient's decision-making capacity assessment, and that patient was advised to call 911 again if condition worsens.

14.3 PCR Writing Standards

  • Objective: Describe what you see, hear, smell. "Patient appears intoxicated" (subjective) vs "Patient has slurred speech, unsteady gait, odor of alcohol on breath" (objective).
  • Medical Terminology: Use proper terms (dyspnea not "short of breath", diaphoretic not "sweating"). Spell correctly.
  • Chronological: Events in order they occurred. Use timestamps (1430: arrived on scene, 1432: began assessment, 1435: started IV, etc.).
  • Legibility: If handwritten, must be readable. Illegible PCRs may be returned for re-writing.
  • No Abbreviations (except standard): Use standard medical abbreviations (BP, HR, IV, etc.). No text-speak or shorthand.
  • Corrections: If error, draw single line through, write "error", initial, and write correct info. NEVER scribble out or use white-out (looks like you're hiding something).

14.4 PCR Deadline

PCR must be completed and submitted before end of shift. Exception: Multi-patient MCI — PCRs due within 24 hours.

Section 15Field Training Program

15.1 FTO Program Structure

All new EMS personnel complete 8-week FTO program after academy graduation:

Phase Duration Focus
Phase 1 2 weeks Observation: FTO demonstrates all procedures. Trainee observes, asks questions, familiarizes with equipment/protocols.
Phase 2 3 weeks Guided Practice: Trainee performs patient care with FTO coaching. FTO provides immediate feedback and correction.
Phase 3 3 weeks Independent Performance: Trainee operates as primary provider. FTO observes silently, only intervenes if patient safety at risk. Trainee makes all decisions.

15.2 FTO Evaluation

FTO completes Daily Observation Report (DOR) after each shift rating trainee on:

  • Patient assessment skills
  • Treatment interventions (appropriate, performed correctly)
  • Decision-making (transport decisions, medication choices)
  • Communication (patient rapport, radio, hospital handoff)
  • Professionalism (attitude, uniform, punctuality)
  • Stress management (calm under pressure, multi-tasking)

Rating scale: 1 (unacceptable) to 5 (exceeds standards). Trainee must average 4.0+ to pass.

15.3 FTO Extension or Failure

  • If trainee struggling, FTO may request extension (additional 2-4 weeks).
  • If trainee fails to meet standards after extension, terminated from EMS. May re-apply after 6 months.
  • Automatic failure events: Patient harm due to trainee error, scope of practice violation, dishonesty, insubordination, repeated unsafe driving.

15.4 Ongoing Training

All EMS personnel complete 40 hours annual continuing education:

  • ACLS/PALS recertification (every 2 years)
  • Trauma refresher (PHTLS or equivalent)
  • Medical protocols update training (annual)
  • De-escalation / mental health crisis intervention
  • Infection control / HIPAA compliance
  • Quarterly skills check-offs (intubation, IV, cardiac arrest scenarios)

Section 16Internal Affairs & Disciplinary Actions

16.1 Levels of Discipline

Level Violation Examples Penalty
Level 1 (Verbal Counseling) Minor uniform violation, late to shift (first offense), incomplete PCR (minor details) Verbal warning from Supervisor. Documented in personnel file.
Level 2 (Written Reprimand) Repeat Level 1 violations, unprofessional conduct with patient, vehicle not stocked Written reprimand. Remedial training. Suspension of promotion eligibility for 6 months.
Level 3 (Suspension) Medication error (non-fatal), HIPAA violation, reckless driving, neglect of duty Suspension without pay (3-14 days). Mandatory retraining. Probation 6-12 months.
Level 4 (Demotion) Serious scope of practice violation, patient neglect, repeated Level 3 offenses Reduction in rank. Loss of ALS certification (if applicable). Extended probation.
Level 5 (Termination) Patient harm due to gross negligence, practicing outside scope, falsifying PCR, theft (drugs/equipment), patient abuse Immediate termination. Loss of state EMS certification. Possible criminal prosecution.

16.2 Internal Affairs Investigations

  1. Complaint: Patient complaint, supervisor report, or peer report filed with Medical Director.
  2. Investigation: Medical Director or Assistant Director investigates. Reviews PCRs, radio traffic, interviews witnesses and accused personnel.
  3. Personnel Rights: Accused personnel notified of complaint within 48 hours. May have union rep present during interview.
  4. Findings: Sustained (violation occurred), Not Sustained (insufficient evidence), Unfounded (no violation), Exonerated (actions were appropriate).
  5. Discipline: If sustained, Medical Director determines discipline level. Personnel may appeal to EMS Board.

16.3 Mandatory Reporting

The following must be reported to Medical Director immediately:

  • Patient death on EMS watch (including refusals who later died)
  • Medication errors
  • Ambulance collisions
  • Equipment failures that delayed care
  • Scope of practice violations witnessed
  • HIPAA breaches
  • Patient complaints about EMS care
